DSC 106 Project 2

Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

One World Bank dataset, two very different stories about whether internet access drives economic prosperity.

Jingwen Gu, Leo Wiemelt, Raymond Wang

jig040@ucsd.edu, wiemelt@ucsd.edu, rywang@ucsd.edu

Proposition

Expanding internet access is essential to economic prosperity.

Data source: World Bank Human Development Indicators, using the infrastructure and economy-and-growth categories.

For the Proposition

Internet access and income levels rise together

Design Decisions and Rationale

  • Score: -1.0 We aggregated countries into four income groups instead of showing country-level data. That smoothing hides within-group variation and makes the gap between rich and poor countries look cleaner and more stable than the underlying country distribution really is.
  • Score: -0.5 We framed the chart as a long-run trend from 2000 to 2019. This encourages viewers to read the separation between income groups as persistent evidence that connectivity and prosperity naturally go together, even though the chart does not prove that one caused the other.
  • Score: +2.0 We used direct labels on the lines and added the x-axis label Year with whole-number ticks. Those choices make the chart easier to read without forcing the viewer to match colors back to a separate legend or decode awkward decimal formatting.
  • Score: +1.5 We kept the y-axis starting near zero and used a simple percentage scale for internet use. That is a relatively earnest choice because it preserves the magnitude of the differences instead of exaggerating them with a truncated axis.
  • Score: -1.0 We titled the chart "The most prosperous countries have also remained the most connected," which nudges the viewer toward reading the pattern as confirmation that wealth and connectivity naturally reinforce one another. The title is not factually false, but it makes a correlational pattern sound more conclusive and aligned with the proposition than a more neutral title would.

Against the Proposition

Access gains do not reliably translate into GDP growth

Design Decisions and Rationale

  • Score: -1.0 We reframed the proposition around change from 2009 to 2019 instead of country levels. This is persuasive because it asks whether internet expansion actually paid off economically in a specific decade, which is a much tougher test than a cross-sectional snapshot.
  • Score: +1.0 We kept both axes in familiar percentage units and added zero reference lines. That makes it easy to see that many countries expanded access while posting weak or even negative GDP growth, without requiring viewers to decode transformed scales.
  • Score: -1.0 We labeled a small set of counterexamples such as Oman, Kuwait, Iran, and Brunei Darussalam. These annotations make the negative cases more memorable, while we leave unlabeled many countries where internet access and GDP moved upward together.
  • Score: 0.0 We used region color to show that the weak change relationship is not confined to one place. This keeps the visualization from feeling like it relies on a single outlier, even though the highlighted examples still guide the reader toward the anti-proposition interpretation.
  • Score: -1.5 We used the title "From 2009 to 2019, internet expansion did not reliably produce economic growth," which frames the chart in near-causal language even though the plot only shows an inconsistent association between changes in two variables. That wording is persuasive because it sounds decisive, but it overstates what the evidence alone can establish.

Final Reflection

Designing both sides around the same dataset made it clear that persuasion often comes more from framing. The strongest contrast in this project came from switching between income-group averages over time and country-level change over a single decade. In the first visualization, internet access and prosperity look tightly linked because the grouped lines show a clean, persistent separation between richer and poorer countries. In the second visualization, that same story weakens dramatically once the question becomes whether countries that expanded internet access the most also saw the strongest GDP per capita growth from 2009 to 2019.

What surprised us most was how defensible both views could feel even when they led to opposite conclusions. We now think ethical visualization is not just about avoiding obvious lies; it is also about being honest about what framing choices leave in or leave out. Persuasive choices feel acceptable when they remain readable, technically justified, and transparent enough that a careful reader could still reconstruct the broader context. They become misleading when framing, annotation, or selective emphasis hides equally plausible interpretations while still appearing authoritative at first glance.
